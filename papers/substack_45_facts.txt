45 Facts Beat a Trillion Tokens

I ran a simple experiment. I took the smallest viable language model — qwen2.5-coder at 3 billion parameters — and gave it a high school biology test. It scored 58%. Not bad for a coding model taking a biology exam.

Then I connected it to Sara Brain, the cognitive architecture I've been building. Sara is a persistent knowledge graph — facts stored as traceable paths in SQLite, not compressed into neural network weights. I hand-taught Sara 45 biology facts in about 30 minutes. Things like "directional selection is selection for one extreme phenotype" and "memory cells are immune cells that remember previously encountered pathogens."

Score with Sara: 80%.

That beats GPT-3.5, which scores around 70% on the same benchmark. GPT-3.5 has roughly 175 billion parameters trained on trillions of tokens. Sara has 45 sentences in a database file smaller than a profile photo.

I also tried the automated approach. I had the 3B model read Wikipedia and extract biology facts on its own. It ingested 28,000 neurons worth of knowledge. The score dropped to 51%. More knowledge made it worse.

Why? Because the 3B model hallucinated during extraction. It read "CH4 (one carbon atom bonded to four hydrogen atoms)" and extracted "methane has 4 carbon atoms." Sara stored it faithfully. One wrong fact in the right context is worse than no fact at all.

Then I built a teacher loop. Sara takes the test, sees what she got wrong, examines her own activation patterns, and tries to teach herself the gap. Starting from zero she climbed to 60% — then crashed to 35% when the accumulated bad corrections overwhelmed the good ones. The 3B model generated things like "transmitted using muscle cells" as correction facts. The automated teacher poisoned the student.

The human-taught version never had this problem. Every fact was correct because a human who understood the subject wrote it. The architecture was identical in every experiment. The only variable was who did the teaching.

This is the paper's argument in one line: AI systems need teachers, not training data.

Training compresses trillions of tokens into opaque weight matrices. No individual fact is inspectable or correctable. Hallucinations are structural — pattern collisions from lossy compression. You cannot fix them by adding more data. More data means more patterns, more collisions, more hallucination surface area.

Teaching stores one fact at a time as an explicit, traceable path. Wrong facts are diagnosable and fixable. "Methane has 4 carbon atoms" can be found, traced to its source, and removed. In a trained model, the same error is invisible — distributed across billions of parameters with no address.

The second paper is on Zenodo now. Two papers in ten days. Both built on a Mac Mini with no GPU, using the smallest viable model, as an independent researcher.

The brain doesn't need a GPU. It doesn't need a datacenter. It doesn't need a trillion tokens.

It needs a teacher.
