These pairing probabilities represent the likelihood of specific nucleotides forming bonds, which can be treated as vectors in a mechanical system. The "mass" in this scenario is the mass of the RNA nucleotides, which influences how they respond to these forces. "Acceleration" is the change in velocity of the nucleotides as they move to form or break bonds.

  

### 5.6.3 Pairing Probabilities as Forces

  

Pairing probabilities in RNA are analogous to forces in mechanical systems. These probabilities determine the attraction between nucleotides, driving them to move from one position to another. For example, a high pairing probability between two nucleotides indicates a strong attractive force, causing the nucleotides to move closer together to form a bond. Conversely, a low pairing probability indicates a weaker force, resulting in less movement.

  

### 5.6.4 Velocity and Acceleration in RNA Movement

  

\*\*Velocity\*\* is the rate at which an RNA nucleotide changes its position. It is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction. In RNA mechanics, velocity can be understood as the speed at which a nucleotide moves towards or away from another nucleotide due to the attractive or repulsive forces (pairing probabilities).

  

\*\*Acceleration\*\* is the rate of change of velocity over time. In the context of RNA, acceleration occurs when the velocity of a nucleotide changes due to varying forces acting upon it. For instance, if a nucleotide initially moves slowly towards another nucleotide but then speeds up as it gets closer, it is experiencing acceleration.

  

### 5.6.5 Applying Newton's Second Law to RNA

  

To apply Newton's Second Law to RNA, consider the following steps:

  

1\. \*\*Identify the Forces\*\*: Determine the pairing probabilities between nucleotides, which act as the forces driving their movement.

2\. \*\*Calculate the Mass\*\*: Consider the mass of the RNA nucleotides, which influences how they respond to the forces. Heavier nucleotides will have more inertia and will require more force to achieve the same acceleration as lighter nucleotides.

3\. \*\*Determine Acceleration\*\*: Use the equation F = ma to calculate the acceleration of the nucleotides. This involves dividing the force (pairing probability) by the mass of the nucleotide.

  

### 5.6.6 Example: RNA Folding Dynamics

  

Imagine an RNA molecule with nucleotides A and U that have a high pairing probability, indicating a strong attractive force. According to Newton's Second Law, this force will cause the nucleotides to accelerate towards each other. As the nucleotides move closer, their velocity increases, and they eventually form a stable bond.

  

Conversely, if an external force (such as a change in temperature) acts on the RNA molecule, it can disrupt the existing bonds. This external force can be considered a positive axial force, causing the nucleotides to accelerate away from each other, leading to the unfolding of the RNA structure.

## 5.7 Newton's Third Law of Motion

### 5.7.1 Newton's Third Law of Motion and RNA Mechanics

  

Newton's Third Law of Motion states that for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. This fundamental principle of classical mechanics can be directly applied to understand the behavior of RNA molecules, particularly in terms of their interactions and the forces they exert on each other. This law is crucial for understanding the dynamic behavior of RNA, including how it folds, maintains stability, and responds to external stimuli.

  

### 5.7.2 Understanding Action and Reaction in RNA

  

In the context of RNA, Newton's Third Law can be observed in the interactions between nucleotides and the forces they exert on each other. When one nucleotide exerts a force on another, such as during the formation or breaking of hydrogen bonds, an equal and opposite force is exerted in return. This principle helps explain the dynamic behavior of RNA molecules, as the forces between nucleotides constantly push and pull, leading to changes in the molecule's structure.

  

### 5.7.3 Hydrogen Bond Formation and Breaking

  

Hydrogen bonds are a key component of RNA structure, providing the necessary interactions for the formation of secondary structures such as hairpins and loops. When two nucleotides form a hydrogen bond, they exert forces on each other. According to Newton's Third Law, the force exerted by one nucleotide on the other is matched by an equal and opposite force exerted by the second nucleotide. This balance of forces is what keeps the hydrogen bond stable.

  

Conversely, when a hydrogen bond breaks, the forces that were holding the nucleotides together are released. The nucleotide that was pulling on the other will experience an equal and opposite force pushing it away. This release of forces can lead to changes in the RNA structure, such as unfolding or transitioning to a different configuration.

  

### 5.7.4 RNA Folding and Unfolding

  

RNA folding and unfolding are dynamic processes that involve the constant formation and breaking of hydrogen bonds. Newton's Third Law is evident in these processes, as the forces exerted during bond formation and breaking are always balanced by equal and opposite forces. This balance of forces is crucial for maintaining the stability of RNA structures and ensuring that they can transition smoothly between different configurations.

  

For example, consider an RNA hairpin structure. When the hairpin forms, the nucleotides at the base of the hairpin exert forces on each other to create hydrogen bonds. These forces are balanced by equal and opposite forces, resulting in a stable hairpin structure. If an external force, such as a change in temperature, causes the hairpin to unfold, the breaking of hydrogen bonds will release the forces, leading to a new configuration.

  

### 5.7.5 External Forces and RNA Stability

  

External forces can disrupt the balance of forces within an RNA molecule, leading to changes in its structure. According to Newton's Third Law, any external force acting on the RNA will be met with an equal and opposite reaction force. This reaction force can cause the RNA to change its configuration, either by breaking existing bonds or forming new ones.

  

For instance, if a ligand binds to an RNA aptamer, the binding force will be met with an equal and opposite reaction force from the RNA. This interaction can cause the RNA to change its structure to accommodate the ligand, leading to a new stable configuration. Understanding these interactions is crucial for designing RNA-based sensors and therapeutic agents that can respond to specific stimuli.

  

### 5.7.6 Practical Implications

# 6 Description of the Serena RNA Analysis Tool

The Serena RNA Analysis Tool is an advanced research instrument designed to optimize RNA aptamer designs by providing a comprehensive analysis of RNA structures. This tool integrates computational models with experimental data to evaluate the performance of RNA aptamers, focusing on their stability, binding affinity, and switching capabilities. The Serena tool leverages several new metrics to provide deeper insights into RNA behavior, including Ensemble Variation (EV), Local Minima Variation (LMV), Weighted Structures (WS), and Comparison Structures (CS). Each of these metrics offers a unique perspective on the stability and functionality of RNA aptamers, enabling researchers to make more informed decisions during the design process.

  

Ensemble Variation (EV): Ensemble Variation (EV) is a metric designed to measure the instantaneous variation within an RNA ensemble. Inspired by Nupack’s Ensemble Defect, EV addresses some of the limitations of the original metric by providing a more accurate representation of variation at 1 to 2 kcal from the Minimum Free Energy (MFE). This metric is particularly useful for assessing the stability of RNA folds, as it considers the number of nucleotides and the number of structures within the ensemble. A lower EV value indicates a more stable RNA fold, while a higher EV value suggests greater instability.

Local Minima Variation (LMV): Local Minima Variation (LMV) measures the amount of variation within a subset of an RNA ensemble relative to a reference secondary structure. This metric is particularly useful for identifying local minima within the ensemble, which can provide insights into the stability and functionality of RNA aptamers. LMV values are expressed in units of Ensemble Variation (EV). LMV has multiple flavors, including LMV\_C (for weighted structures), LMV\_R (for the first structure in the subset), and LMV\_M (for the unbound MFE structure). A high LMV value indicates greater variation from the reference structure, suggesting a lack of similarity to the local minima, while a low LMV value indicates less variation.

  

Weighted Structures (WS): Weighted Structures (WS) represent the average and most common nucleotide pairing in a discrete range of the ensemble, given in dot-bracket notation. This metric looks at each nucleotide position for each alternative secondary structure in a range of the ensemble and finds the most common binding. WS replaces the MFE as a more accurate representation of the ensemble and the most likely forming of the RNA. If the weighted structure does not convert into pairs cleanly, it indicates that the RNA is intrinsically unstable.

  

Comparison Structures (CS): Comparison Structures (CS) is a novel metric that provides a single structural representation of two ensembles mashed together to compare and contrast them. This metric is used to represent whether a weighted structure is more like a second state weighted/MFE structure or a first state MFE structure. CS helps in understanding the similarities and differences between secondary structures in a human-readable way, as well as being able to be parsed by code. This metric is crucial for evaluating the switching capabilities of RNA aptamers.

  

Sub-metrics within Comparison Structures: The Comparison Structures metric contains several sub-metrics that provide further granularity in the analysis of RNA aptamers. These sub-metrics are derived from the comparison of RNA ensembles and include Bound to Unbound Ratio (BURatio), Both Raise (both\_Raise), Bound Raise (BRaise), Unbound Drop (UDrop), Both to Total Ratio (BothtoTotal), Bound to Total Ratio (BoundToTotal), Unbound to Total Ratio (UnboundToTotal), and Bound to Both Ratio (Bound\_both). These sub-metrics offer detailed insights into the behavior of RNA structures under different conditions, allowing for a more nuanced understanding of their performance.

  

Bound to Unbound Ratio (BURatio): The Bound to Unbound Ratio (BURatio) is a sub-metric that measures the ratio of bound nucleotides to unbound nucleotides in the current energy group comparison structure. This ratio provides insights into the binding affinity of the RNA aptamer, indicating how effectively it can transition between bound and unbound states.

  

Both Raise (both\_Raise): Both Raise (both\_Raise) is a sub-metric that measures the increase in the number of nucleotides that are both bound and unbound from the last group to the current group. This metric helps in understanding the dynamics of RNA folding and the stability of the aptamer in different states. The "both" metrics refer to nucleotide positions with the same structural configuration present in both the bound and unbound weighted structures used in the comparison.

  

Bound Raise (BRaise): Bound Raise (BRaise) is a sub-metric that measures the increase in the number of bound nucleotides from the last group to the current group. This metric provides insights into the binding efficiency of the RNA aptamer and its ability to maintain a stable bound state. "Bound" refers to structural positions found only in the bound weighted structure.

  

Unbound Drop (UDrop): Unbound Drop (UDrop) is a sub-metric that measures the decrease in the number of unbound nucleotides from the last group to the current group. This metric helps in understanding the transition dynamics of the RNA aptamer and its ability to switch from an unbound to a bound state effectively. "Unbound" refers to structural positions found only in the unbound weighted structure.

  

Both to Total Ratio (BothtoTotal): Both to Total Ratio (BothtoTotal) measures the ratio of nucleotides that are both bound and unbound to the total number of nucleotides in the sequence. This ratio provides a comprehensive understanding of the RNA aptamer's performance and stability under different conditions.

Bound to Total Ratio (BoundToTotal): Bound to Total Ratio (BoundToTotal) measures the ratio of bound nucleotides to the total number of nucleotides in the sequence. This metric offers insights into the overall binding efficiency of the RNA aptamer.

  

Unbound to Total Ratio (UnboundToTotal): Unbound to Total Ratio (UnboundToTotal) measures the ratio of unbound nucleotides to the total number of nucleotides in the sequence. This metric helps in understanding the RNA aptamer's ability to maintain an unbound state when necessary.

Bound to Both Ratio (Bound\_both): Bound to Both Ratio (Bound\_both) measures the ratio of bound nucleotides to nucleotides that are both bound and unbound. This metric provides insights into the balance between bound and unbound states, offering a detailed understanding of the RNA aptamer's switching capabilities.

## 6.2 Explanation of Metrics from Eterna Wetlab Measurements

  

In the study of RNA aptamers, several key metrics are used to evaluate their performance and functionality. These metrics include the Eterna Switch Subscore, Eterna Baseline Subscore, Eterna Folding Subscore, Eterna Score, KDOFF, KDON, and Fold Change. Each of these metrics provides unique insights into different aspects of RNA aptamer behavior, enabling researchers to optimize their designs for specific applications.

  

The Eterna Switch Subscore quantifies the fold-increase in KD,obs (observed dissociation constant) between the OFF and ON states of an RNA aptamer. This metric indirectly measures the range of switching at the optimal MS2 concentration, providing insights into the aptamer's ability to transition between states. A higher Switch Subscore indicates a more effective switch, which is crucial for applications that require precise control over RNA behavior.

  

The Eterna Baseline Subscore quantifies how close the affinity of the ON state is to that of the native MS2 hairpin. This metric is essential for understanding the baseline performance of the RNA aptamer in its active state. A higher Baseline Subscore suggests that the aptamer closely mimics the native MS2 hairpin, which is important for maintaining functionality in various applications.

  

The Eterna Folding Subscore rewards RNA aptamers that properly fold the MS2 hairpin. This metric decreases if the maximum signal falls below a certain threshold, indicating poor folding performance. A higher Folding Subscore signifies that the RNA aptamer can reliably form the desired secondary structure, which is critical for its stability and functionality.

  

The Eterna Score is a composite metric that adds the Switching Subscore, Baseline Subscore, and Folding Subscore. This combined score captures the three main qualities of a good switch: effective switching, strong baseline performance, and proper folding. A higher Eterna Score indicates a well-designed RNA aptamer that excels in all three areas, making it suitable for various applications.

  

KDOFF measures the affinity of the RNA aptamer to the OFF state, with higher values indicating greater fold change. This metric is important for understanding how well the aptamer can maintain its inactive state. KDON, on the other hand, measures the affinity to the ON state, with lower values indicating greater fold change as it is used as a comparison with the base MS2 affinity. Together, KDOFF and KDON provide a comprehensive view of the RNA aptamer's switching dynamics.

  

Fold Change is calculated as the ratio of KD,obs in the OFF state to KD,obs in the ON state. This metric provides a direct measure of the RNA aptamer's switching efficiency. A higher Fold Change indicates a more effective switch, which is crucial for applications that require precise control over RNA behavior. By understanding these metrics, researchers can optimize RNA aptamer designs to achieve the desired performance and functionality.

# 9 Detailed Description of the Molecular Snare Observations

  

The concept of the molecular snare in RNA aptamers is a sophisticated mechanism designed to detect and bind specific molecules, forming a loop around the detected molecule in the bound state. This system is composed of two primary components: an independent static stem and the triggering molecule binding sites. The molecular snare is a crucial subsystem within the larger aptamer structure, playing a vital role in its functionality.

  

The first component of the molecular snare is the independent static stem, which can often be the 5'3' static stem. This static stem provides structural stability to the aptamer, ensuring it can withstand the mechanical forces experienced during the transition between unbound and bound states. The static stem's stability is essential for the proper functioning of the molecular snare, as it anchors the binding sites and allows them to form a loop around the target molecule.

  

The second component is the triggering molecule binding sites. These sites are initially connected to the static stem and are stretched out in the unbound state, temporarily binding to hybrid stems to form what is referred to as the "tab." When the RNA aptamer encounters the molecule it is designed to detect, the binding sites are pulled away from the temporary hybrid stems. This action initiates the formation of a loop that wraps around the detected molecule, effectively trapping it.

  

When the RNA aptamer comes upon the target molecule, the binding sites or "tabs" are drawn inward, away from the temporary hybrid stems. This inward pull creates tension within the RNA strand, causing a shift in the secondary structure of the aptamer. The binding sites then engage with the target molecule, forming a stable loop that secures the molecule in place. This process is crucial for the aptamer's ability to detect and report the presence of specific molecules.

  

The mechanics of the molecular snare are further illustrated by the Magnet Stem concept used in the winning design of the OpenTB round and is being used for the development of a tuberculosis sensor.. This concept is essentially a molecular snare, where the static stem and binding sites work together to trap the target molecule. The Magnet Stem provides additional support and stability, enhancing the aptamer's performance and increasing its fold change potential. The molecular snare's ability to form a loop around the detected molecule is a key feature that enables the aptamer to function as a molecular switch.
